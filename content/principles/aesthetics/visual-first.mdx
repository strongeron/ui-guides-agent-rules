## Why This Matters

Most interfaces over-rely on text. Feature sections become walls of paragraphs.
Icons are tiny afterthoughts with long explanations beneath them. Users don't
read—they scan. When text dominates, the experience becomes dense and exhausting.

Visual-first design flips this ratio. Large, bold visuals communicate the concept
instantly. Text appears sparingly—a single word, a short label, essential context
only. The result is immediate comprehension and memorable impact.

## Best Practices

- Let visuals (icons, illustrations, color, space) carry 90% of communication
- Reduce text to essential labels and single words when possible
- Make icons and visual elements large and bold, not tiny afterthoughts
- If you need a paragraph to explain something, the visual design has failed
- Create visual hierarchy through scale, color, and position—not more text
